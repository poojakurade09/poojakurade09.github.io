Auditing the Asset Lifecycle
in a Global DAM Ecosystem

Overview

A global marketing organisation had been running their DAM platform for 4+ years — but without governance, taxonomy, or a defined asset lifecycle. Growth had been organic and chaotic: duplicate files, empty metadata, abandoned folders, and no single owner of the platform.

I was engaged to audit the current state end-to-end, map how assets were actually created, stored, searched and shared, and produce an actionable prioritisation framework to fix the most critical gaps.

Context & Approach

A DAM Nobody Trusted

Teams had built workarounds — private Dropbox folders, WhatsApp threads, email attachments — because the platform had failed them enough times that they stopped using it. Six Systemic Problems

Problem 01
No Taxonomy or Naming Convention

Folders were named by campaign, by team, by date, by person — sometimes all four. 63% of assets had no meaningful filename. Search was effectively broken.

Problem 02
Metadata Fields Left Empty

The platform had 14 metadata fields. On average, assets had only 1.8 filled. Without tags the search algorithm returned results ordered by upload date — useless in practice.

Problem 03
No Defined Asset Lifecycle

Assets were uploaded but never reviewed, updated, or archived. Files 7 years old were still appearing in search results alongside current campaign materials.

Problem 04
Duplicate Assets at Scale

The audit identified 4,200+ duplicate or near-duplicate files — 35% of total library volume. Teams were re-uploading the same assets rather than searching for existing versions.

Problem 05
No Clear Platform Ownership

The DAM was nobody's job. IT supported the infrastructure; marketing used it — but no team owned governance, training, or platform strategy.

Problem 06
Shadow Systems Proliferating

Teams built workarounds — Dropbox folders, local drives, WhatsApp threads. The DAM was being bypassed because emailing a file was faster than finding one in the platform.

Recommendations

Recommendations Framework

The findings pointed to four interconnected pillars — fixing only one without the others would have limited lasting impact. Renaming files without fixing metadata governance, for instance, solves search for a month before the chaos returns.

Pillar 01
Taxonomy & Naming

Build a structured folder hierarchy and a consistent naming convention that is intuitive, scalable, and enforced at the point of upload.

  • Define top-level folder categories by content type
  • Create [Brand]–[Campaign]–[AssetType]–[Version] naming standard
  • Archive legacy folders without disrupting active work
Pillar 02
Metadata Schema

Redesign the metadata structure with required fields, controlled vocabularies, and smart defaults — making it faster to tag correctly than to skip it.

  • Reduce 14 fields to 6 required + 4 optional
  • Introduce controlled lists for Asset Type, Region, Campaign
  • Auto-populate date, uploader, and format on upload
Pillar 03
Governance & Ownership

Establish clear roles, responsibilities, and workflows so the platform has people — not just technology — accountable for quality.

  • Appoint a DAM Owner with dedicated capacity
  • Define upload, review, and archive approval workflows
  • Establish a quarterly audit cadence with reporting
Pillar 04
Training & Adoption

Change management is as important as platform configuration. Users need to trust the system before they'll abandon their workarounds.

  • Create role-specific onboarding guides (Creator / Consumer)
  • Run hands-on training sessions for each team
  • Build a self-service FAQ and naming convention reference
Collaboration

Workshop & Next Action Points

Research findings were presented to 12 stakeholders in a 3-hour alignment workshop. Rather than presenting a top-down solution, the workshop was structured to let the team co-prioritise the fixes — increasing buy-in and surfacing constraints that interviews hadn't revealed.

Reflection

What I Learned

This project reinforced how often technology gets blamed for problems that are fundamentally organisational. The DAM platform wasn't the issue — the lack of ownership, process, and accountability around it was. The platform could do everything the teams needed; they just hadn't set it up to.

The hardest part wasn't identifying the problems — the content audit made those undeniable. The hard part was helping stakeholders see that a quick fix (renaming a few folders) wouldn't solve a structural issue. Getting buy-in for the governance piece required showing that the technical problems they experienced were downstream of an ownership problem upstream.

If I were to do this again, I would run the workshop earlier in the process — before the full audit. The priority matrix exercise surfaced constraints that would have changed which interviews I prioritised and which platform analytics I examined first.
