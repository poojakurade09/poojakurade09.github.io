TTK Prestige Home Appliance —
Website Revamp

Overview

TTK Prestige, one of India's most trusted kitchen appliance brands, needed a complete digital overhaul. The existing website suffered from poor product discoverability, an outdated UI, and a purchase flow that was actively losing customers.

This project involved end-to-end UX research, information architecture restructuring, and a full visual redesign — transforming how millions of users discover and purchase Prestige products online.

Background

The Challenge

TTK Prestige is one of India's most iconic kitchen and home appliance brands — trusted by millions of households for over 70 years. Despite strong offline brand equity, the existing e-commerce website was failing to convert digital-first shoppers. Navigation was fragmented, the mobile experience was broken, and the visual identity felt dated compared to modern D2C competitors.

I was brought in to redesign the end-to-end website experience — restructuring the information architecture, modernising the visual system, and building a more intuitive purchase journey that reflected the brand's true premium positioning.

Research

Understanding the Problem

Before sketching a single screen, the team conducted a comprehensive audit of the existing website alongside user interviews and competitor benchmarking to understand where the current experience was failing.

Pain Point 01
Couldn't Find Products Fast Enough
Users couldn't find products or key information quickly. The existing navigation was deep, inconsistently labelled, and buried product lines that users were actively searching for — leading to high drop-off at the category level.
Pain Point 02
No Way to Filter, Browse or Compare
The website lacked meaningful discovery mechanisms — no guided filters, no contextual recommendations, and no visual hierarchy to move users from browsing to purchase intent. Over 68% of traffic came from mobile, yet the browsing experience was fragmented on small screens.
Pain Point 03
Website Felt Untrustworthy Online
Inconsistent typography, outdated visuals, and absent social proof created a credibility gap. Users trusted the Prestige brand name but not the website — and regularly completed purchases on third-party marketplaces instead.

Gathering Insight

The research phase combined qualitative and quantitative methods to build a complete picture of user behaviour and expectations.

I conducted 8 user interviews with frequent online shoppers aged 24–55, ran a heuristic evaluation against Nielsen's 10 principles, and reviewed Google Analytics data to identify the highest drop-off points in the purchase funnel.

Key Decisions from Wireframe Testing
Three things we changed after early feedback
1
Category page — filter placement. First wireframe had filters in a horizontal bar above the grid. Users in testing missed it on mobile. Moved to a collapsible left sidebar on desktop and a slide-up drawer on mobile — significantly improving filter usage in the final design.
2
Product detail page — image gallery position. Initial wireframe placed the image gallery below the product title and price. Testing showed users wanted to see the product visually before reading specs. Moved the gallery to a fixed left column — a layout confirmed in the final PDP screens.
3
Checkout — single page vs. stepped flow. We wireframed both. A stepped 3-screen checkout felt safer to users during testing — especially for first-time buyers who wanted to review details before committing. The single-page version was kept only for returning users via a "Fast Checkout" shortcut.

What This Project Delivered

1
Fixing the IA unlocked everything downstream. Products buried 3+ levels deep were the single biggest drop-off driver. Surfacing category navigation at the top level directly drove the bounce rate reduction — users could reach what they came for in one step instead of four.
2
Mobile-first is non-negotiable for India's e-commerce. With 68% of traffic on mobile, treating mobile as an afterthought was costing direct conversions. Designing the PDP information hierarchy for mobile first — then expanding to desktop — changed the layout decisions at every level.
3
Trust signals convert as much as design does. Adding certification badges, seller verification, and estimated delivery dates felt like small additions. In post-launch feedback they consistently came up as reasons users chose to complete the purchase on the Prestige site rather than defaulting to Amazon or Flipkart.

Beyond metrics, the redesign delivered a scalable design system and component library — reducing future design and development time for new campaigns and product launches.

Reflection

What I Learned

Working on TTK Prestige taught me how to navigate the tension between a strong heritage brand identity and the demand for a modern, digital-native experience. The biggest challenge wasn't the design itself — it was aligning stakeholders who had different mental models of what the website should do.

I also gained deeper experience in designing for India's diverse user base — where connectivity varies, where many users switch between desktop and mobile mid-session, and where trust signals like phone numbers and certification badges still play a meaningful role in purchase confidence.

If I were to revisit this project, I'd push for usability testing earlier in the wireframe stage. Some layout decisions that seemed intuitive turned out to be counterintuitive for users, and catching them earlier would have saved revision cycles down the line.
