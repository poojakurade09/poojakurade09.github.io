JIO-BP Fuel Booking App

Overview

Jio-BP aimed to improve the fuel booking experience by reducing wait times, improving station discoverability, and enabling a smoother digital-first journey for customers.

The objective was to design a mobile app experience that simplifies fuel booking, improves trust, and supports scale across multiple cities and fuel stations.

Background

The opportunity

Jio-BP — a joint venture between Reliance Industries and BP — operates one of India's largest networks of mobility stations. Despite strong brand presence, the fuel booking experience was entirely offline: customers drove to stations with no way to check wait times, compare fuel prices, or reserve a slot in advance.

The business opportunity was clear: a well-designed mobile app could digitise the booking flow, reduce queues at stations, and build long-term loyalty through rewards. I was brought in as Lead UX/UI Designer to design the end-to-end experience from research to high-fidelity prototype.

Research

Understanding the Problem

I started with a one-week discovery sprint combining user interviews, stakeholder sessions, and competitive analysis to map the full picture before any design work began.

8 User interviews with frequent fuel customers
3 Station manager sessions to understand operational pain
4 Competitor apps reviewed (HP Pay, BPCL Smartdrive, PetroCard)

I reviewed four competitor apps to identify gaps and opportunities before designing a single screen.

Gap identified: no competitor offered pre-booking with real-time confirmation. This became Jio-BP's core differentiator.

Four recurring themes emerged across all sessions:

Insight 01
No visibility into nearby stations or wait times

Users couldn't find or compare stations before leaving home — leading to frustration and wasted trips.

6 of 8 users had driven to a full station
Insight 02
Booking flow lacked clarity and confidence

Users were unsure what to expect during and after a booking — no clear confirmation or status tracking.

Insight 03

Turning insights into direction

I reframed each research finding as a "How Might We" question — these became the north star for every wireframe and design decision.

HMW 01

How might we make nearby station availability transparent — so users can plan before leaving home, not after arriving at a full queue?

→ Led to: map-first home screen, real-time station status on card

HMW 02

How might we design a booking flow that gives users clear confirmation and live status tracking at every step?

→ Led to: booking status chip, countdown on confirmation screen

HMW 03

How might we reduce dependency on staff for routine bookings — freeing them to handle exceptions instead of every transaction?

→ Led to: self-service booking flow, digital queue management

HMW 04

How might we onboard first-time users quickly and confidently — so they understand the value before making their first booking?

→ Led to: 3-screen value prop onboarding, progressive disclosure in registration

Ideate

Wireframes & User Flow

Before touching colour or typography, I mapped the core user journey through low-fidelity wireframes. The early focus was on two competing approaches: a map-first view versus a station-list view for finding nearby fuel. After testing rough sketches with 4 participants, the map-first layout won — it reduced decision time by giving spatial context at a glance.

Key decisions made at wireframe stage: collapsing the side navigation behind a hamburger to keep the home screen focused, surfacing fuel type and price on the station card so users didn't need to drill in, and placing the booking CTA persistently at the bottom to minimise scroll.

Design

Screens & Flows

From onboarding to booking confirmation — every screen designed to reduce friction and build user confidence.

01  ·  Onboarding

Design decision: Each onboarding card leads with the emotional payoff — find a station, book fuel, earn rewards — rather than feature lists. Research showed users committed to signing up only after seeing tangible benefit upfront. Single focus per screen ensures the message lands at a glance.

02  ·  Registration

Design decision: The registration flow is split across four discrete screens to keep each step cognitively lightweight. Separating phone verification, terms acceptance, and profile setup lets users recover from errors on a single step without losing progress — a pattern that reduced drop-off in testing.

03  ·  Core Flows

Design decision: The home screen leads with the nearest station and today's fuel price — the two pieces of information users said they checked first. Side navigation is tucked behind a hamburger to keep the home screen focused; the Nearby map surfaces stations in spatial context rather than as a list, reducing decision time.

04  ·  Booking & Dashboard

Design decision: The booking confirmation screen uses a large status chip prominently above the fold — the single most-tested element. The dashboard consolidates fuel spend, rewards balance, and upcoming bookings in scannable cards so repeat users always know where they stand.

What Changed After Testing

1
Map-first beats list-first for proximity tasks. Early versions used a station list — user testing showed 6 of 8 participants oriented faster with the map view. We pivoted at the wireframe stage and never looked back.
2
Confirmation anxiety is real. In round 1, users didn't trust the booking was complete without a visible status chip. Adding "Booking Confirmed" with a countdown increased perceived reliability and removed the most common support query scenario.
3
Onboarding is high-stakes for a transactional app. Users who skipped onboarding struggled with the rewards system later in the session. The 3-screen value prop flow was validated as essential, not decorative.
Reflection

What I Learned

This project sharpened my ability to move fast without losing depth. A few things I'd approach differently next time:

Learning 01
Test earlier, with rougher artefacts

We waited until mid-fidelity wireframes to start testing. In hindsight, even paper sketches with 2–3 users would have surfaced the map vs. list insight a week earlier and saved us a round of rework.

Learning 02
Design for edge cases from day one

Error states, empty states, and low-connectivity handling were added late in the sprint. Next time I'd include them in the first wireframe pass — they often reveal interaction problems that the happy path hides.

Learning 03
Brief stakeholders on UX debt early

Some stakeholder-requested features (prominent rewards, station promotions) introduced UX debt we had to negotiate down during reviews. Framing trade-offs as "this now vs. that later" earlier would have sped up alignment.

Constraints & Challenges

Every project has trade-offs. These shaped our decisions:

Challenge 01
4-week concept sprint

A compressed timeline meant we had to prioritise ruthlessly. Discovery, wireframing, and UI all ran in parallel — which required tight communication with the PM and stakeholders to avoid rework.

Challenge 02
Limited access to real users

Recruiting fuel customers for research required coordination with station managers. We supplemented with intercept interviews at two stations to compensate for the small pool.

Challenge 03
Designing for low-connectivity contexts

Fuel station areas often have poor mobile signal. This pushed us to design for graceful degradation — offline state screens, cached station data, and minimal API calls on the booking flow.

Challenge 04
Integrating loyalty without friction

Stakeholders wanted rewards prominently featured, but users in testing found reward tracking interruptive mid-booking. We moved it to the dashboard and post-booking confirmation screen instead.
